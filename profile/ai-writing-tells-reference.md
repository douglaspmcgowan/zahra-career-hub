# AI Writing Tells: Comprehensive Reference

A research-backed guide to what makes text sound AI-generated vs. human-written.
Compiled from academic research, AI detection tool documentation, professional editing guides, and community analysis.

---

## Part 1: The Science -- How AI Detection Actually Works

AI detectors primarily measure two things:

**Perplexity (word-level predictability)**
How "surprised" a language model would be by the text. AI models select statistically likely next words, producing text with low perplexity -- smooth, predictable, uniform. Human writing has higher perplexity because humans make unexpected word choices, use slang, switch registers, and go on tangents. (Source: GPTZero documentation)

**Burstiness (sentence-level variation)**
How much sentence length and complexity varies across a document. Human writing is naturally "bursty" -- a three-word fragment, then a 40-word run-on, then a mid-length sentence. AI text has low burstiness because the model uses the same statistical process for every sentence, producing remarkably uniform length and structure. (Source: GPTZero, academic studies on stylometric detection)

**What this means in practice:** To sound human, you need unpredictable word choices AND uneven sentence rhythms. AI text fails on both counts -- it is simultaneously too smooth at the word level and too uniform at the structure level.

Academic research (2024-2025) confirms: AI-generated texts demonstrate lower perplexity, more uniform sentence structures, and higher lexical repetitiveness compared to human-written texts. Sentences in AI text cluster between 18 and 22 words, producing mechanically flat rhythm. Human sentences range widely and shift based on communicative goals like emphasis, pacing, and emotional beats.

## Part 3: Structural Patterns That Scream AI

### 1. The Rule of Three (Tricolon Abuse)

AI compulsively groups things in threes with parallel grammar:

- "Clear, concise, and compelling"
- "Innovative, efficient, and transformative"
- "Plan, execute, and iterate"
- "From research to strategy to implementation"

**Why it's a tell:** Humans use tricolons sometimes -- "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" -- but AI does it in virtually every paragraph. One tricolon is rhetoric. Three back-to-back tricolons is a machine.

**The fix:** Use two items. Or four. Or one strong one. Break the pattern.

### 2. Negative Parallelism -- "It's Not X, It's Y"

- "It's not about working harder -- it's about working smarter."
- "This isn't just a tool -- it's a mindset shift."
- "Success isn't about perfection -- it's about progress."

**Variation: "Not X. Not Y. Just Z."**
- "Not ambition. Not ego. Just a person who never learned to say enough."

**Why it's a tell:** This construction sounds profound while saying nothing. It's AI's go-to move for creating false depth. Humans rarely frame things this way in spontaneous writing.

### 3. The Setup-Payoff / Rhetorical Question Pattern

- "The result? Devastating."
- "The goal? Simple."
- "What does this mean for marketers? Everything."
- Rhetorical question, then "The answer lies in..."

**Why it's a tell:** AI uses self-posed questions as a crutch for transitions. Humans transition more loosely -- or just start the next thought without announcing it.

### 4. Em-Dash Overload

AI -- especially Claude -- uses em dashes constantly:
- For parenthetical asides -- like this one -- that could be commas
- For dramatic pauses -- creating artificial tension
- For list introductions -- replacing colons
- For restatements -- saying the same thing differently

**Why it's a tell:** A typical human essay might have 2-3 em dashes. AI text often has 15-20+. Claude is particularly notorious for this.

### 5. Uniform Paragraph Length

AI paragraphs are eerily similar in length -- typically 3-5 sentences each, all roughly the same word count. Real writing has:
- One-sentence paragraphs for punch
- Long sprawling paragraphs when developing an idea
- Uneven rhythm that matches the writer's thought process

### 6. Fractal Summaries / Signposted Structure

AI tells you what it's going to say, says it, then tells you what it said:
- "In this article, we'll explore..."
- [content]
- "In conclusion, we've explored..."

It does this at every level -- document, section, and paragraph. Each paragraph often has its own mini-intro and mini-conclusion.

### 7. The Listicle in a Trench Coat

AI disguises numbered lists as prose:
- "The first step is to research your market. The second step is to draft your positioning. The third step is to test your messaging."

Real prose doesn't announce its own structure this way.

### 8. Superficial Analysis via "-ing" Phrases

AI appends vague significance to sentences using present participles:
- "...highlighting the importance of collaboration."
- "...reflecting broader trends in the industry."
- "...underscoring the need for innovation."
- "...showcasing the team's commitment to excellence."

**Why it's a tell:** These phrases add zero information. They're the written equivalent of nodding sagely.

### 9. "Despite Its Challenges..." Formula

AI follows a rigid pattern for balanced analysis:
- Acknowledge positives
- Pivot: "Despite its challenges..." or "However, it's not without limitations..."
- End optimistically: "...the future remains promising."

This mechanical balance is a strong AI fingerprint.

### 10. Anaphora Abuse (Repetitive Sentence Openings)

AI repeats the same sentence starter multiple times:
- "They assume that users will pay. They assume that developers will build. They assume that the market will grow."

One repetition is emphasis. Four is a machine.

### 11. Bold-First Bullets

Every bullet point starts with a **bolded keyword** followed by a colon or explanation. This formatting pattern is extremely common in AI output and rare in natural human writing (outside of technical documentation).

### 12. False Ranges

"From X to Y" constructions where the endpoints don't form a real spectrum:
- "From innovation to implementation to cultural transformation"
- "From strategy to execution to lasting impact"

### 13. Invented Concept Labels

AI creates compound terms and treats them as established concepts:
- "the supervision paradox"
- "the acceleration trap"
- "the alignment framework"

These sound academic but are made up on the spot.

## Part 4: Tone and Voice Patterns

### Over-Formality

AI defaults to formal register because it was trained on Wikipedia, news articles, and academic papers. It writes "utilize" instead of "use," "facilitate" instead of "help," "in order to" instead of "to."

### Compulsive Balance / Both-Sides Hedging

Every claim gets qualified:
- "While X is true, it's also important to consider Y."
- "On one hand... on the other hand..."
- "This approach has many benefits, though it's not without limitations."

Claude is particularly known for this -- it's trained to avoid overconfidence, which produces writing where every assertion is immediately softened.

### Enthusiasm Inflation

AI makes everything sound exciting and important:
- A minor CSS fix becomes "a groundbreaking improvement"
- A meeting recap becomes "a transformative discussion"
- A simple tool becomes "a powerful, innovative solution"

Real humans are selectively enthusiastic. AI is uniformly enthusiastic.

### The Absence of Negativity

AI rarely criticizes, complains, expresses frustration, or takes strong positions. It's trained to be helpful and balanced, which means it defaults to positivity even when the topic calls for directness or bluntness. Real writing has opinions. AI writing has "perspectives to consider."

### People-Pleasing Tone

AI writes like someone trying not to offend anyone:
- "This is a great question!"
- "There are many valid approaches..."
- "It really depends on your specific situation..."

### Self-Referential Meta-Commentary

AI comments on its own process:
- "Let me break this down..."
- "I should note that..."
- "It's worth mentioning that..."
- "To be transparent..."

Humans just say the thing. They don't announce that they're about to say the thing.

## Part 5: What Human Writing Does Differently

### Uneven Rhythm

Real sentences vary wildly in length. Short. Then a longer one that builds and develops an idea across multiple clauses. Then medium. Then a fragment. The rhythm matches thought, not a template.

AI sentences cluster between 18-22 words. That's the dead zone. If your paragraph has five sentences and they're all roughly the same length, it reads as artificial.

### Genuine Specificity

Human writing names things:
- Not "a leading tech company" but "Google"
- Not "a popular coastal city" but "San Francisco"
- Not "improved key metrics" but "cut load time from 4.2s to 1.1s"

AI is vague because it's hedging. It says "various tools and technologies" because it doesn't know which ones you actually used.

**The resume test:** "Could 1,000 people copy-paste this exact line into their own resume?" If yes, it's AI-level generic.

### Imperfection and Messiness

Real writing has:
- Sentence fragments. On purpose.
- Parenthetical asides that don't quite fit (but you left them in anyway)
- Changed direction mid-paragraph because a better idea showed up
- Contractions, colloquialisms, slang
- Occasional grammar "mistakes" that are actually voice (starting sentences with "And" or "But")
- Varied punctuation -- sometimes a dash, sometimes ellipsis, sometimes just ending abruptly

AI writing is suspiciously perfect. No typos, no awkward phrasing, no rough edges. That perfection itself is a tell.

### Personal Experience and Concrete Detail

Humans write from memory:
- "I once spent three hours debugging a CSS issue that turned out to be a missing semicolon"
- "My apartment in Berkeley has this tiny kitchen where I do all my meal prep on a cutting board balanced on the stove"
- "The interview was at 9am and I'd been up since 5 rehearsing answers in the shower"

AI writes from patterns:
- "Many developers have experienced the frustration of debugging challenging issues"
- "Working in compact living spaces requires creative solutions"
- "Interview preparation involves thorough practice and preparation"

The human version has weight. The AI version could apply to anyone.

### Opinions and Strong Positions

Humans pick sides:
- "This framework is overengineered garbage for 90% of use cases"
- "I genuinely love this tool and use it every day"
- "The hiring process at most companies is broken and everyone knows it"

AI hedges:
- "This framework has both advantages and disadvantages depending on the use case"
- "This tool offers many useful features that some users may find valuable"
- "The hiring process presents both opportunities and challenges for candidates and employers alike"

### Digressions and Tangents

Human writers go off-topic, circle back, mention something unrelated. AI stays rigidly on-topic because it's optimizing for relevance at every token.

### Varied Register and Tone Within a Single Piece

A human might start formal, crack a joke, get serious again, use slang, then end with something thoughtful. AI maintains a uniform register throughout because it's optimizing for consistency.

## Part 6: AI Tells Specific to Resumes, Bios, and Portfolios

### Resume Red Flags

**Generic objective/summary statements:**
- "Results-driven professional seeking to leverage dynamic skillset in a challenging environment"
- "Passionate and dedicated team player with a proven track record of success"

**Vague accomplishments:**
- "Improved processes and increased efficiency"
- "Managed various projects and initiatives"
- "Utilized multiple tools and technologies"

**The fix:** Every bullet should pass the "Could 1,000 people use this exact line?" test. Replace vague claims with specific metrics, tools, and outcomes.

**AI resume example:**
> "Spearheaded cross-functional initiatives to streamline operational workflows, resulting in enhanced productivity and stakeholder satisfaction across multiple business units."

**Human resume example:**
> "Rebuilt the onboarding process for new hires -- cut training time from 14 days to 6 by replacing the 80-page manual with a Notion wiki and weekly check-ins."

### Bio / Portfolio Red Flags

**The "passionate professional" opener:**
- "I'm a passionate marketing professional with a deep commitment to driving results..."
- "As a dedicated and innovative problem-solver..."

**The skills laundry list:**
- "Skilled in project management, data analysis, strategic planning, team leadership, and cross-functional collaboration"

**The empty mission statement:**
- "I strive to make a meaningful impact by leveraging my diverse skillset to create innovative solutions that drive growth and transform organizations."

**AI bio example:**
> "Jane is a results-oriented marketing professional with a proven track record of developing comprehensive strategies that drive engagement and foster meaningful connections. With expertise spanning digital marketing, content creation, and data-driven insights, she is passionate about leveraging innovative approaches to deliver transformative results."

**Human bio example:**
> "I got into marketing sideways -- started as a biology research assistant, realized I was better at explaining the research than doing it, and ended up writing grant applications that actually got funded. Now I do the same thing for brands: figure out what's actually interesting about what they do and help them say it clearly. Based in Berkeley. Usually working from a coffee shop."

The human version has a story. It has specificity. It has personality. It has an actual voice. The AI version could be anyone.

## Part 7: Before/After Examples

### Example 1: Professional Summary

**AI version:**
> "I am a highly motivated and results-driven professional with extensive experience in marketing and communications. I leverage data-driven insights to develop comprehensive strategies that enhance brand visibility and drive meaningful engagement. My passion for innovative solutions, combined with my expertise in cross-functional collaboration, enables me to deliver transformative results in fast-paced environments."

**Problems:** "results-driven," "leverage," "data-driven insights," "comprehensive strategies," "enhance," "meaningful engagement," "passion," "innovative solutions," "cross-functional collaboration," "transformative results," "fast-paced environments." Eleven AI flags in three sentences.

**Human rewrite:**
> "I've spent the last four years figuring out how to get people to actually read marketing emails (open rates went from 12% to 34% at my last job -- turns out subject lines matter more than anyone wants to admit). I'm good at the analytical side but I'm better at the creative side. Looking for a role where I can do both."

### Example 2: Project Description

**AI version:**
> "This project involved developing a comprehensive web application that streamlines the user experience by leveraging cutting-edge technologies. The robust platform features a seamless interface that empowers users to navigate complex workflows with unprecedented efficiency, showcasing innovative design principles and best-in-class performance optimization."

**Problems:** "comprehensive," "streamlines," "leveraging," "cutting-edge," "robust," "seamless," "empowers," "navigate," "unprecedented," "showcasing," "innovative," "best-in-class." Twelve flags. Says nothing specific about what the app actually does.

**Human rewrite:**
> "Built a job tracking app in Next.js so my friend could stop using a spreadsheet to manage her applications. It pulls listings from a few APIs, lets her save and annotate them, and sends a weekly digest email. Nothing fancy -- the main thing was making it actually usable on her phone since she does most of her browsing there."

### Example 3: Cover Letter Opening

**AI version:**
> "I am writing to express my enthusiastic interest in the Marketing Coordinator position at Acme Corp. With my proven track record in developing data-driven marketing strategies and my passion for fostering meaningful brand connections, I am confident that my comprehensive skillset aligns perfectly with your organization's mission to deliver innovative, results-oriented solutions."

**Human rewrite:**
> "I saw the Marketing Coordinator posting on LinkedIn and it's the first job listing in weeks that made me actually stop scrolling. I've been doing freelance marketing for biotech startups since graduating, and the way your team talks about making complex science accessible is exactly the problem I've been solving -- just on a smaller scale."

### Example 4: Skills Description

**AI version:**
> "Possesses a robust skillset encompassing advanced data analytics, strategic planning, and cross-functional team leadership. Demonstrates unwavering commitment to leveraging innovative methodologies to drive operational excellence and foster sustainable growth across diverse business units."

**Human rewrite:**
> "I'm strongest at data analysis -- SQL, Python, the usual. I can pull reports, but more importantly I can explain what the numbers mean to people who don't care about numbers. I've also run a few small teams (3-5 people) and I'm honest about the fact that I'm still learning the management side."

### Example 5: "About" Section

**AI version:**
> "In today's fast-paced digital landscape, organizations face unprecedented challenges in navigating the complexities of modern marketing. Our team serves as a beacon of innovation, leveraging cutting-edge strategies to unlock new possibilities and empower brands to thrive in an ever-evolving marketplace."

**Human rewrite:**
> "Marketing got weird in the last few years. The old playbook doesn't work and half the new advice is just people selling courses. We help companies figure out what's actually worth doing and skip the rest. Mostly B2B. Mostly companies with 50-200 employees who don't have a huge marketing team yet."

## Part 8: Claude-Specific Patterns

Claude (the model generating this document, for full transparency) has its own distinctive tells:

1. **Em-dash addiction** -- Claude uses em dashes far more than ChatGPT or humans. Nearly every paragraph will have at least one parenthetical aside set off by em dashes.

2. **Compulsive hedging** -- "I think," "it seems," "from what I can tell," "it's worth noting." Claude is trained to avoid overconfidence, which produces text that constantly qualifies itself.

3. **Self-referential commentary** -- "I should note that," "to be transparent," "I want to be clear that." Claude narrates its own reasoning process.

4. **Over-organization** -- Headers, bullets, numbered lists, bold keywords. Claude structures everything even when a flowing paragraph would be more natural.

5. **Both-sides-ism** -- Every assertion gets a counterpoint. Claude is trained to be balanced, which means it rarely takes a strong position without immediately softening it.

6. **Longer, clause-heavy sentences** -- Claude builds complex sentences with multiple dependent clauses, using em dashes (there it is again) to manage the complexity.

7. **Predictable paragraph rhythm** -- Long contextual sentence, shorter key claim, clarifying sentence with nuance. This pattern repeats.

## Part 9: Quick-Reference Checklists

### Red Flags Checklist (Does Your Text Do This?)

- [ ] Most sentences are 18-22 words (uniform length)
- [ ] Paragraphs are all roughly the same size
- [ ] Uses "delve," "landscape," "tapestry," "robust," "seamless," or "leverage"
- [ ] Contains "it's important to note" or "it's worth noting"
- [ ] Uses "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not X -- it's Y"
- [ ] Groups things in threes repeatedly
- [ ] Starts conclusion with "In conclusion," "Overall," or "In summary"
- [ ] Uses "serves as," "stands as," or "marks" instead of "is"
- [ ] Contains 5+ em dashes
- [ ] Every bullet starts with a **bolded keyword**
- [ ] No contractions anywhere
- [ ] Zero grammar imperfections
- [ ] No specific names, numbers, or concrete details
- [ ] Uniformly positive tone
- [ ] Uses "comprehensive," "innovative," or "transformative"

### Human-Sounding Checklist (Does Your Text Do This?)

- [ ] Sentence lengths vary dramatically (5 words to 35 words)
- [ ] At least one sentence fragment or intentionally "broken" structure
- [ ] Uses contractions naturally
- [ ] Contains at least one specific name, number, or concrete detail
- [ ] Has a moment of opinion or strong position
- [ ] Includes at least one unexpected word choice or turn of phrase
- [ ] Tone shifts at least once (serious to light, formal to casual)
- [ ] Contains a personal detail or anecdote
- [ ] Paragraphs are different lengths
- [ ] Doesn't announce its own structure ("In this section, we'll explore...")
- [ ] Uses "is" and "has" instead of "serves as" and "boasts"
- [ ] Avoids all words from the 32-word shortlist above
