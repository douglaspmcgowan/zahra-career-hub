# Interview Prep Guide for Zahra

How to present yourself, answer common questions, and tell your story effectively. This guide is built specifically from your background and strengths.

---

## Your Story in 60 Seconds

Memorize a version of this. Adjust the ending based on what role you're interviewing for.

> "I'm an Australian biologist and D1 athlete who's spent the last four years in the US on full-ride scholarships — first at the University of Buffalo where I studied biological sciences with a focus in neuroscience, and now at UC Berkeley where I'm finishing a certificate in entrepreneurship while competing in high jump. I've been coaching track and field for eight years, I've worked in education and sales, and I'm passionate about work that's both intellectually challenging and deeply people-oriented. I'm looking for a role in the Bay Area where I can combine my science background with my interpersonal skills and continue growing. And as an Australian citizen, I'm eligible for the E-3 visa, which makes the sponsorship process simple and fast for employers."

**Key points to always hit:**
1. Biology degree + entrepreneurship certificate (shows range)
2. D1 athlete at two universities (shows discipline and elite performance)
3. 8 years of coaching (shows sustained leadership and people skills)
4. E-3 visa (removes the sponsorship concern immediately)

## The 10 Questions You'll Definitely Get

### 1. "Tell me about yourself."

Use the 60-second story above. Then pivot to why you're excited about THIS specific role.

**Don't:** List your resume chronologically.
**Do:** Tell a story with a theme — you're someone who combines scientific thinking with genuine care for people.

---

### 2. "Why do you want to work here?"

**Framework:** Research + Connection + Contribution

> "I've been following [Company] because [specific thing you noticed — a product, mission, article, or project]. What drew me in is [connection to your values or background]. And I think I can contribute [specific skill or perspective] because of my experience in [relevant area]."

**Example for Lawrence Hall of Science:**
> "I've been to Lawrence Hall and saw how you make science genuinely exciting for kids. That's exactly what I want to do — take complex science and make it accessible and fun. With my biology degree, eight years of coaching young people, and my own experience as an athlete who had to learn how to teach different people in different ways, I think I'd thrive in that environment."

**Example for a biotech company:**
> "What excites me about [Company] is that you're working at the intersection of biology and [marketing/AI/technology]. I have a biology degree and I understand the science behind what you do, but I also have marketing and communication training from UC Berkeley. I can translate complex science into clear messaging, and I genuinely enjoy the relationship-building side of this work."

### 3. "What are your strengths?"

**Your top 3 (with evidence):**

**1. Connecting with people quickly**
> "I can read a room and adapt how I communicate. As a coach, I had to figure out within minutes what approach would work with each kid — whether they needed encouragement, firm boundaries, or just to be heard. In sales, I had to adjust my pitch based on who walked up. This is something I've practiced for years, and it comes from studying neuroscience and psychology — I understand why people respond the way they do."

**2. Thriving under pressure and challenge**
> "I've competed at NCAA Nationals. I've broken records. I've maintained my academics while training as a Division 1 athlete. I chose neuroscience because it was the hardest option. I chose to transfer to Berkeley for one final season because I wanted to test myself at the highest level. I'm drawn to challenge, not scared of it."

**3. Building culture and bringing people together**
> "At Buffalo, I was team captain and I transformed the culture — event groups that never talked started hanging out, people held themselves to higher standards, and the whole team's morale shifted. I did that by showing up every day with energy, organizing social events, and running groups where we talked about purpose beyond sport. People gave back the energy I put in."

---

### 4. "What's your greatest weakness?"

**Pick something real but frame it as growth:**

> "I can be too patient sometimes. Coming from a coaching and education background, I'm trained to give people time and space to figure things out. But in a faster-paced professional environment, I'm learning that sometimes you need to be more direct and move things forward rather than waiting for someone to get there on their own. It's something I'm actively working on."

**Or:**

> "I don't have a lot of formal corporate experience. Most of my professional life has been in coaching, education, and athletics. But I've found that the skills transfer directly — managing a classroom of disruptive students is honestly harder than most office situations. And I'm a fast learner who's not afraid of being new at something."

### 5. "Tell me about a time you dealt with conflict."

**Use the Educational Assistant story:**

> "When I was working as an educational assistant at a high school in Perth, my entire role was de-escalating kids who were being disruptive or aggressive. One thing I learned quickly is that there's a very short window before a student decides they don't like you and shuts down completely. So I learned to lead with questions, not commands. Instead of telling a kid to stop, I'd ask them what was going on. Usually, they just wanted to be heard. Once they felt understood, they became way more receptive. I'd adjust my tone, my body language, my approach — every kid was different. By the end of my time there, I had strong relationships with some of the most difficult students because I'd earned their trust by genuinely trying to understand them instead of just managing them."

### 6. "Tell me about a time you led a team."

**Use the Buffalo Captain story:**

> "I was team captain of the University at Buffalo track and field team during the 2024-25 season. When I started, the team was pretty fragmented — each event group kind of kept to themselves and there wasn't a strong sense of unity. I made it my mission to change that. I organized social events outside of training, I made a point to spend time with every event group, and I started running optional weekly gatherings where we'd talk about things beyond sport — purpose, identity, gratitude. Over the course of the year, the culture shifted noticeably. People treated each other better, held themselves to higher standards, and the team became genuinely close. My coaches and teammates noticed the difference. It was one of the most meaningful things I've done."

### 7. "Why should we hire you?"

**Framework:** Unique combination + immediate value + low risk

> "I bring a combination that's hard to find — a biology degree with real scientific training, marketing and business education from UC Berkeley, and eight years of experience working with and leading people. I'm not just someone who 'likes people' — I have trained interpersonal skills backed by neuroscience and psychology education, refined through years of coaching, teaching, and competing at the Division 1 level. And practically, I'm already in Berkeley, ready to start, and eligible for the E-3 visa — which means hiring me is as simple as hiring any American candidate, with almost no extra cost or paperwork."

---

### 8. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

**Be honest about growth, not specific titles:**

> "I want to be deep into a career where I'm constantly learning and making an impact on the people around me. Whether that's leading a team, running programs, or growing into a senior role at a company whose mission I care about — the specifics are less important to me than being challenged and doing meaningful work. I chose to come to the Bay Area because this is where I see the most opportunity for that kind of growth."

---

### 9. "Do you need visa sponsorship?"

**This is your secret weapon. Deliver it confidently:**

> "I'm Australian, so I'm eligible for the E-3 visa. It's exclusive to Australians and works very differently from the H-1B. There's no lottery, no cap issues — about half the E-3 slots go unused every year. The process is fast, usually just a few weeks, and the employer's only requirement is filing a Labor Condition Application, which is the same basic form used for any work visa. It's renewable indefinitely. So practically speaking, sponsoring me is faster, cheaper, and more certain than sponsoring almost any other international candidate."

**If they seem hesitant:**
> "I'd be happy to share some information about the E-3 process with your HR team. Most immigration attorneys are very familiar with it, and many employers have told me it's easier than they expected."

---

### 10. "Do you have any questions for us?"

**Always ask at least 2. Good ones:**

- "What does success look like in this role in the first 6 months?"
- "What's the team culture like? How do people collaborate?"
- "What's the biggest challenge your team is facing right now?"
- "Is there room for growth in this role? What does the career path look like?"
- "What do you personally enjoy most about working here?"

**Never ask about salary, vacation, or benefits in a first interview.** Save those for when they make an offer.

## Behavioral Interview Framework: STAR Method

For any "Tell me about a time when..." question, structure your answer as:

- **S**ituation — Set the scene briefly (1-2 sentences)
- **T**ask — What was your responsibility or goal?
- **A**ction — What did YOU specifically do? (This is the longest part)
- **R**esult — What happened? Quantify if possible.

- A: Read each customer quickly, adapted pitch for age/gender/personality, built comfort and trust in short interactions

## Industry-Specific Prep

### If Interviewing for Biotech Marketing
- Know the company's main products and who they sell to
- Be ready to explain: "I can understand the science AND communicate it to non-scientists"
- Mention your neuroscience literature review experience — you can read papers
- Ask about their go-to-market strategy or how marketing and science teams collaborate

### If Interviewing for Science Education
- Talk about making science FUN, not just accurate
- Emphasize your coaching experience — you already know how to teach
- Mention your psychology training — you understand how people learn
- Ask about their approach to informal education and how they measure impact

### If Interviewing for Summer Camps
- Be enthusiastic, energetic, and warm — this is about personality
- Emphasize safety, reliability, and experience with kids
- Talk about your coaching and educational assistant work
- Ask about the age range, daily schedule, and training program

### If Interviewing for AI Companies
- You don't need to be technical — they need communicators and marketers
- Frame yourself as "science-literate" — you can understand what the engineers build
- Emphasize your marketing training and community/relationship-building skills
- Ask about the team you'd be on and what their biggest communication challenge is

### If Interviewing for Sales/Business Development
- Lead with your sales pop-up experience and how you read people
- Connect your coaching and teaching to sales — both require adapting to individuals
- Mention your negotiation coursework from UC Berkeley
- Ask about their sales process, target market, and how success is measured

## Before Every Interview — Checklist

- [ ] Research the company (website, recent news, Glassdoor reviews)
- [ ] Know the job description inside and out — highlight 3 requirements you match
- [ ] Prepare your 60-second story (adjust the ending for this role)
- [ ] Pick 2-3 STAR stories most relevant to this role
- [ ] Prepare your E-3 visa explanation
- [ ] Prepare 2-3 questions to ask them
- [ ] Dress one level above the company's dress code
- [ ] Arrive 10 minutes early (or test your video call setup 15 minutes early)
- [ ] Bring 3 printed copies of your resume (for in-person)
- [ ] Send a thank-you email within 24 hours

## Phrases to Use

These are high-impact phrases that match Zahra's authentic voice and resonate with interviewers:

- "I'm drawn to challenge, not scared of it."
- "I don't just like people — I've trained myself to understand them."
- "I led by example, and the team gave back the energy I put in."
- "I ask questions instead of making assumptions about people."
- "How you deliver what you say matters as much as what you say."
- "I want to do work that challenges me to keep learning for the rest of my life."
- "As an Australian citizen, the E-3 visa makes this simple for you."

## Phrases to Avoid

- "I just really like people" — too vague. Say WHY and HOW.
- "I'm a hard worker" — everyone says this. Show it through your stories.
- "I'll do anything" — shows desperation, not flexibility. Say "I'm excited about [specific thing]."
- "I don't have much experience" — reframe as "My experience is in coaching, education, and athletics, and those skills transfer directly."
- "I need this job to stay in the country" — never lead with need. Lead with value.
